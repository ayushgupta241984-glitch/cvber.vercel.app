## INTERVIEW

### Q1: Tell me about your art. What do you create and where do you share it?

**Jordan:** I do editorial illustration — magazine covers, book covers, that kind of work. About 8K followers on Instagram but the real work comes from direct client relationships. I have a personal site with a portfolio, and I'm on Twitter where I share process shots. I've been doing this full-time for seven years. It's my career, not a side hustle.

### Q2: How did you get into this? What keeps you going?

**Jordan:** I studied illustration at SVA, worked at a design studio for two years, then went freelance. What keeps me going is the creative control. When a client hires me for a book cover, I get to set the visual tone for someone's entire story. That's meaningful work. The money is decent — $55K isn't rich but it's stable. The part that keeps me up at night is the protection side. That's where I lose sleep.

### Q3: What's the best thing that's happened with your art this year?

**Jordan:** I got a book cover commission for a mid-size publisher. $4,500 project, ran for three months. The author was thrilled, the publisher wants me back. That's the kind of work I want more of. But here's the thing — that cover design? I found it on a print-on-demand site three months later. Someone had screenshotted the Amazon listing and was selling prints of my cover art. On Etsy. For $15.

### Q4: Has anyone ever used your work without permission? Tell me what happened.

**Jordan:** Three times this year. The book cover was the worst one. I spent three months on it, the publisher paid me $4,500, and then someone is selling prints of it on Etsy for $15. I contacted the seller — they ignored me. I filed an Etsy IP complaint — it took six weeks to resolve. During that time they sold about 300 prints. The other two were editorial pieces I did for magazines. Found them on Pinterest being used as "inspiration boards" — which is basically a nice way of saying they stole my work.

### Q5: How did that make you feel? What did you do about it?

**Jordan:** Professional anger. Not emotional — strategic. I documented everything. Screenshots with timestamps, URLs, the works. Filed the Etsy complaint, sent DMCA notices to Pinterest. The Etsy one eventually worked but it took six weeks and I had to follow up four times. The Pinterest ones are still up. I have a folder on my desktop called "theft documentation" with 47 screenshots from this year alone. That's not正常 — that's a system that's broken.

### Q6: What do you worry about when it comes to your art being stolen or copied?

**Jordan:** Two things. First, the volume. It's getting worse. AI tools make it easier to copy styles. I've seen people generate "in the style of [artist name]" and get convincing results. That's my style being cloned without my consent. Second, the legal framework. DMCA is from 1998. It's 2026. The internet has changed completely but the law hasn't. I'm operating with 28-year-old tools in a real-time world.

### Q7: If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about how your art is protected, what would it be?

**Jordan:** Automated enforcement. I don't want to find theft — I want to be notified when it happens. I don't want to file DMCAs — I want them filed automatically. I don't want to follow up — I want escalation handled. I want a system that does what a lawyer would do but at scale, automatically, for free or cheap. I've done the math — a lawyer on retainer costs $3,600/year. I can't justify that on $55K income. But I can justify $300/year for a tool that does the same thing.

### Q8: What do you currently do to protect your work? Any tools, services, habits?

**Jordan:** I register copyrights. $55 per work through the copyright office. I've registered 3 pieces this year — the book cover and two editorial pieces. Total cost: $165. I also have a clause in my contracts that transfers copyright to me until final payment. And I use lower-resolution images on my portfolio. That's it. It's not enough but it's what I can afford and manage.

### Q9: How much time and money have you spent on this in the last year?

**Jordan:** Money: $165 on copyright registration. Time: maybe 30 hours total. That includes researching options, filing DMCAs, documenting theft, following up on complaints. At my hourly rate of $75, that's $2,250 in time. So total cost of protection: $2,415. For three pieces. That's absurd.

### Q10: What's worked? What hasn't? Why?

**Jordan:** Copyright registration works — if you get to court. It gives you statutory damages and attorney fees, which makes lawyers willing to take the case. But getting to court is the problem. DMCA works about 40% of the time — small sites comply, big sites ignore you. Watermarks work for deterrence but look unprofessional. Nothing works at scale. I need something that works automatically across the entire internet.

### Q11: Imagine a free tool that did everything — DMCA, blockchain proof, watermarking, AI detection — all in one. What's your first reaction?

**Jordan:** [nods slowly] Free. Okay. I'm listening. My first thought is: what's the business model? If it's free, how do they sustain it? Are they selling data? Are they going to pivot to paid? I need to understand the economics before I trust it. But the concept — yes. That's exactly what I've been looking for. The question is execution.

### Q12: What would make you trust it? What would make you suspicious?

**Jordan:** Trust: transparent business model. Tell me how you make money. Open source the core technology. Have a public track record. Show me the team — who built this, what are their credentials. Suspicious: "AI-powered" without explaining what that means. No team page. No business model transparency. Free with no clear path to sustainability. Privacy policy that's vague about data usage.

### Q13: If you used it and it found someone had stolen your art, what would you want it to do?

**Jordan:** First: notify me with evidence. Screenshot, URL, timestamp. Second: auto-file DMCA with the hosting provider AND the domain registrar AND the platform. Third: track the takedown — did they comply? If not, escalate. Fourth: maintain a legal record — if I need to pursue this in court, I have documentation. Fifth: if it's a recurring offender, flag it and prioritize enforcement. I want a complete workflow, not just detection.

### Q14: What would need to be true for you to tell a friend about it?

**Jordan:** It would need to work for me for at least three months. I'd need to have filed at least one successful takedown through it. I'd need to understand the business model and trust it. And I'd need it to be affordable — if it's free now but will cost $50/month later, I'd tell my friends to be cautious. I'd also want a professional-grade experience — not something that looks like a weekend project.

### Q15: On a scale of 1-10, how likely are you to sign up for this today?

**Jordan:** 7. I'm interested. I want to understand the business model. I want to see how it works. But the concept is exactly right.

### Q16: What's the one thing that would move that number up?

**Jordan:** Show me the technology. Let me read the documentation. Let me understand how the blockchain proof works, how the watermarking works, how the DMCA automation works. I'm a technical person — I want to see under the hood. If the tech is solid, I'm in.

### Q17: Is there anything else about protecting your art that we haven't talked about?

**Jordan:** The economics of it. Every hour I spend on protection is an hour I'm not creating. At $75/hour, that's real money. A tool that saves me 10 hours/month is worth $750/year to me in recovered创作时间. Even if the tool costs $300/year, I'm still ahead by $450. But most artists don't think about it that way. They think about the sticker price, not the opportunity cost. That's a marketing opportunity — frame it as "save 10 hours/month" not "pay $300/year."
