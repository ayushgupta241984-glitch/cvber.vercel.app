## INTERVIEW

### Q1: Tell me about your art. What do you create and where do you share it?

**Alex:** I do character design for animation — mostly TV shows and web series. About 15K on Instagram, 8K on Twitter. I share process videos, final pieces, behind-the-scenes stuff. I'm at the point where I'm getting consistent work but not at the point where I can be picky. I take what I can get.

### Q2: How did you get into this? What keeps you going?

**Alex:** Art school → internships → freelance. What keeps me going? Honestly? Habit at this point. I've been doing it for five years. It's what I know. The creative part is great. The business side is exhausting. The theft side is... I've stopped caring. That sounds bad but it's true. You can only care so many times before you just... stop.

### Q3: What's the best thing that's happened with your art this year?

**Alex:** I got a character design gig for a web series. $2,500. Good money, good client, good project. But here's the thing — I posted the character on Instagram and within a week it was on three different art accounts reposting my work. With credit, sure. But they're getting the engagement, not me. They're building their audience on my work. And I can't stop it because it's "just reposting."

### Q4: Has anyone ever used your work without permission? Tell me what happened.

**Alex:** [laughs tiredly] How much time do you have? It happens every week. Art accounts repost my work. Sometimes with credit, sometimes without. Print-on-demand sites steal my designs. I found my character on a T-shirt on Temu last month. I reported it. Nothing happened. I posted about it on Twitter. People said "that sucks" and moved on. That's the cycle. It happens, you get mad, you try to do something, nothing works, you move on. Until next week when it happens again.

### Q5: How did that make you feel? What did you do about it?

**Alex:** The first few times? Furious. Devastated. Now? Numb. I've accepted it as the cost of being an artist on the internet. I know that sounds terrible but it's the truth. The system doesn't protect us. DMCA is a joke. Instagram doesn't care. The platforms profit from the stolen content because it drives engagement. Why would they stop it? I've tried everything — reporting, DMCA, public shaming. Nothing works consistently. So I've stopped trying. I just... keep creating.

### Q6: What do you worry about when it comes to your art being stolen or copied?

**Alex:** Honestly? I've stopped worrying. Worrying doesn't help. What I do worry about is the long-term. If AI can generate art in my style, and my art is everywhere online being reposted and studied... eventually, someone can just type "in the style of Alex Rivera" and get something good enough. Then what am I selling? My personality? My brand? Those are fragile things.

### Q7: If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about how your art is protected, what would it be?

**Alex:** Automatic takedowns that actually work. Not "we sent an email." Actual removal. And I want it to be effortless. I don't want to think about it. I want to post my art and know that somewhere, a system is watching and protecting me. Like antivirus for art. You install it and forget about it until it catches something.

### Q8: What do you currently do to protect your work? Any tools, services, habits?

**Alex:** I put my handle on my images. That's it. I used to watermark but it looks bad and clients hate it. I used to post lower-res images but that hurts my portfolio. I've tried everything and nothing is worth the effort. The ROI on protection is negative — it costs more in time and aesthetics than it saves in stolen art.

### Q9: How much time and money have you spent on this in the last year?

**Alex:** Time: maybe 15 hours. Money: $0. But the time is the real cost. Every hour I spend dealing with theft is an hour I'm not creating. And every time I see my art stolen, it takes a piece of my motivation. That's the real cost — the emotional toll.

### Q10: What's worked? What hasn't? Why?

**Alex:** Nothing has worked. DMCA gets ignored. Instagram reports go nowhere. Public shaming gets attention for a day then nothing. Watermarks look bad. Low-res images hurt my portfolio. The system is broken and nobody with power cares enough to fix it. Artists are at the bottom of the food chain.

### Q11: Imagine a free tool that did everything — DMCA, blockchain proof, watermarking, AI detection — all in one. What's your first reaction?

**Alex:** [skeptical] Yeah, I've heard this before. Every few months someone launches a "artist protection tool" and it either dies in a year or turns out to be garbage. I'm not getting my hopes up. But... if it actually worked? If it actually caught stolen art and got it taken down? I'd use it. But I need to see proof first.

### Q12: What would make you trust it? What would make you suspicious?

**Alex:** Trust: longevity. If it's been around for 2+ years and still working. If it has a track record of successful takedowns. If it's used by artists I respect. Suspicious: "AI-powered" marketing. New launch. No track record. Free with no clear business model. Celebrity endorsements (those are paid).

### Q13: If you used it and it found someone had stolen your art, what would you want it to do?

**Alex:** Take it down. Automatically. Without me doing anything. And tell me it happened. I don't want a dashboard. I don't want reports. I want a notification: "We found your art on [site] and removed it." That's it. Simple.

### Q14: What would need to be true for you to tell a friend about it?

**Alex:** It would need to work for me for at least 6 months. I'd need to have had multiple successful takedowns. And I'd need it to be truly effortless — no dashboard to check, no reports to read. Just works in the background.

### Q15: On a scale of 1-10, how likely are you to sign up for this today?

**Alex:** 4. I'm skeptical. I've been burned too many times. Show me it works first.

### Q16: What's the one thing that would move that number up?

**Alex:** A success story from someone I follow. Not a paid promotion. A real artist saying "this actually worked." And it needs to be someone who was skeptical like me — because if a known fanboy says it works, I don't believe them.

### Q17: Is there anything else about protecting your art that we haven't talked about?

**Alex:** The emotional cost. Everyone talks about the money and the time. But the real cost is the motivation. Every time I see my art stolen, a little piece of me dies. And when I try to do something about it and nothing happens, another piece dies. Eventually, I'll stop creating. Not because I can't. Because I don't see the point. That's what theft really costs — not money, but creativity.
